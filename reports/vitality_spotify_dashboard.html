The Partnership Question

Rewards programmes that integrate third-party lifestyle products face a core measurement challenge — do rewards change behaviour, or do they attract members who were already active?

This case study models a hypothetical opt-in partnership between a Vitality-style wellness rewards programme and Spotify Africa, building the analytics layer to evaluate whether music engagement incentives drive measurable improvements in physical activity and member retention.

What Was Measured

The pilot spans 12 weeks — four weeks pre and eight weeks post reward activation — structured to measure directional change at the member cohort level.

  • Did Spotify engagement increase after reward activation?
  • Did physical activity improve among reward-engaged members?
  • What was the retention delta between engaged and non-engaged cohorts?
  • Were there selection bias risks that needed to be controlled?

Analytical Framework

Pilot design with an opt-in reward cohort measured against a non-engaged control group across four measurement dimensions.

  • Engagement metrics:  Spotify streams pre vs post activation
  • Activity metrics:  Weekly exercise events and activity score deltas
  • Retention metrics:  Monthly cohort retention by engagement tier
  • Bias controls:  Opt-in selection bias flagged; baseline equivalence tested

Key Findings

Reward-engaged members showed higher post-pilot activity scores relative to their own pre-pilot baseline.

Retention was notably higher in the engaged cohort at the 8-week mark compared to the control group.

Opt-in members were already more active at baseline — selection bias is a documented confounding factor.

The reward performs best as a retention tool for already-engaged members, not as an activation lever for inactive segments.

Product Implication

A tiered reward structure targeting different engagement segments would sharpen impact and reduce cost-per-active-member ratios.

Inactive segments require a different intervention — the music reward alone is unlikely to move behavioural baselines for disengaged members.

A randomised controlled design in a future pilot would allow causal attribution rather than directional correlation.
